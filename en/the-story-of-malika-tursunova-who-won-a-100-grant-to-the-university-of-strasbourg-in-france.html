Malika Tursunova graduated from the academic lyceum of the international Westminster University in Tashkent.

Currently, she is studying economics at the University of Strasbourg in France.

She won a 100% grant from the Islamic Development Bank and the "Campus France" organization under the French Embassy.

Malika told "Spot" why she chose France, the process of applying for a grant and discounts for students in the country.

Why France

I first heard about studying abroad at the international Westminster in Tashkent. I started thinking when I was studying at the academic lyceum of the university.

The reason for this was the atmosphere in the lyceum. Many people were studying abroad there, and slowly I started to have the same desire.

I really wanted to study in England, but the universities there are very expensive and it was difficult to get a bigger grant. I started studying.

Application processes

Higher education in France (as well as some other European countries) is almost free - you only need to pay 170 euros a year for a bachelor's degree.

The application process for a bachelor's degree lasts from early autumn to December, and for a master's degree from January to February.

You can apply to French universities in Uzbekistan through the "Campus France" organization under the country's embassy.

Photo: from the personal archive of Malika Tursunova

You give them the necessary documents and fill out the "Dossier Blanc" application, which asks you to choose the direction you want to study and 3 universities.

After that, "Campus France" will send these documents to the universities itself.

The documents must be delivered to the "Campus France" organization in person, that is, there is no possibility to submit them online.

The following documents are requested to be submitted to the universities:

  • motivational letter;
  • CV/Resume;
  • school grades. transcripts;
  • certificate;
  • certificate of language proficiency;
  • recommendation letters (for master's degree).

As for language, whether or not knowledge of French is mandatory depends on the program you are applying to.

If you are applying to a program taught in English, knowledge of French is not mandatory.

Each Based on their requirements, the university may request TCF, DELF/DALF for French language programs, and IELTS or TOEFL certificates for English language programs.

In addition, the submitted documents must be translated into the language you choose to study.

100% grant program

The grant I received is a joint grant from the Islamic Development Bank and Campus France. grant.

This grant covers almost all expenses. In particular, tuition and visa costs are covered and a monthly stipend of €700 is provided.

If you live outside the university dormitories, in a rented house and you are not older than 26, the French government will give you additional funds.

For example, I pay €550 a month for rent, so €230 is given to me by the French government just because I am a student and outside the university. he gives it because I live in a rental.

By the way, no financial documents are required to use this privilege, and it is not necessary to be a French citizen.

You submit to the embassy in Uzbekistan:

  • application;
  • a letter of acceptance to the university;
  • a document reflecting the financial situation (parental income or bank account);
  • a document confirming the payment of the visa fee;
  • medical insurance;
  • passport/ID;
  • proof of your place of residence in France document;
  • Air tickets to fly to France (not always required).

If all the documents you submit are reliable and the embassy believes that your financial situation is sufficient, you will most likely get a visa.

It was very easy for me to get a visa because I won a full grant.

Living expenses

There are many discounts for students in France. This allows students to save more money.

For example, I spend 150-250 euros per month on groceries and 320 euros per month on rent because the French government covers half of my rent.

These are my main monthly expenses.

Working as a student. International students in France legally work 20 hours a week. It's possible.

It's about 10 euros an hour here, but after taxes it can be as low as 8 euros.

I'm not working anywhere because the grant I got covers all my expenses, so I'm focusing on studying.

Pros and cons of studying in France

Other cons than being away from home almost none.

One thing I didn't really like was that I couldn't completely choose the subjects to take.

For example, last year I had to study subjects such as law and history, even though my field is economics.

As for the positive aspects, they are many. First of all, I should mention quality education.

Many professors at our university have supported doctoral dissertations. They conduct various researches, participate in high-level conferences, and students have the opportunity to get quality knowledge from them.

The most important thing is that students do not have to spend much on themselves to use these opportunities.

Another good thing is that, as I said earlier, there are always discounts for students in France.

For example, in university canteens for a very low price, i.e. 1 you can eat for a euro. This 1 euro includes a salad, main course and dessert.

There is also a 70% discount on medical services and you do not need to pay for permanent health insurance.

Some services are even free. For example, if I want to go to museums in France, I don't have to pay for it.

And one of the most important and good things here is great career opportunities.

Because there are many famous brands and companies in France, and studying here will give you an advantage when applying for a job in these companies. — Official Data
