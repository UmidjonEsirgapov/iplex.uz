As we all know, the application and admission process for international programs is a process that is completed much earlier than the beginning of the academic year. In this work, speed and diligence are important. Here are some pointers you may find helpful.

Start looking for the right scholarship as early as possible, define your career goals, and work systematically to build your experience, achievements, and qualifications to get a scholarship and study in your chosen country for a master's program. start researching universities and scholarships at least 2, preferably 3 years in advance.

  • Make the most comprehensive list of scholarships you will apply for.

    • There are 2 main sources of scholarships: large public scholarship funds and universities that offer scholarships to international students. When choosing scholarships, find out what the foundations require of you. — Source

    1. Learn what scholarship foundations want from candidates, what achievements they prefer, and use your time as an undergraduate to gain the experience, knowledge and results that will help you get the scholarship you want.
    2. Try to study well. Most scholarships require good academic performance. This does not mean that only excellent students get scholarships. It's not like that. However, the higher the score, the better.
    3. Before you apply for a scholarship, try to define your career path, because your application should describe what you want to achieve in your professional life and how a master's degree will help you succeed in that path.
    4. Use undergraduate courses and theses to strengthen your grant application. Take subjects that are as close as possible to the ones you will study in the master's degree.
    5. Improve your English and pass IELTS or TOEFL in the 3rd year or in the summer before the last year of the bachelor's degree, so that in the 4th year you will not be distracted from preparing the diploma and documents for the master's degree and scholarship.
    6. Contact universities and scholarship funds directly. University admissions and scholarship commissions have special employees who are paid to communicate with potential students and scholarship holders.
